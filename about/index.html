Part 01

Why your site
needs an audit.

Lighthouse doesn't measure how your site looks - it measures what search engines crawl, what visitors on slow connections experience, and what browsers flag before a page even loads. A site can look polished at your desk on a fast connection and fail in every category that actually moves the needle.

Missing security headers are invisible until a browser warning appears. Contrast ratios look fine until you run a WCAG measurement tool. Meta tags seem optional until Google starts ranking you for the wrong keywords - or not at all.

The gap between "looks fine" and "performs well" is exactly where we work. Every issue we've documented was invisible until we measured it.

Each category maps directly to lost revenue, ranking, or legal exposure:

Performance: Every extra second of load time costs roughly 7% of visitors. Google uses page speed as a direct ranking factor. A 60% performance score means your page takes 4-6 seconds on a mobile connection - most people leave by second 3.

SEO: A 65% SEO score means Google is guessing at what half your pages are about. You rank for things you didn't intend, or not at all.

Security: Missing headers (HSTS, CSP, X-Frame-Options) leave you open to clickjacking and script injection. Some gaps trigger browser-level warnings that visitors see before your page loads.

Accessibility: 15-20% of people have a disability that affects how they browse. Beyond the human cost, ADA compliance is increasingly enforced - businesses have been sued over inaccessible websites.

Best Practices: Deprecated APIs and outdated libraries don't break immediately. They accumulate until something stops working and nobody knows why.

Web standards move faster than most development shops update their defaults. Security header requirements have tightened significantly in the last three years. WCAG accessibility rules have been clarified in ways that break patterns that were acceptable in 2020. Chrome and Safari have each shifted what they flag and when.

The site your developer shipped two years ago was built against that era's standards. It hasn't been maintained against this era's. Neither has the platform it's hosted on.

We've audited sites built by reputable agencies that were genuinely excellent when they launched. Every one of them had drifted. Not because of bad work - because time passed.
Part 02

Why automated processes
are often the real culprit.

Your hosting provider, CDN, and any third-party tools running on your domain are all modifying what visitors actually receive - often without your knowledge. They inject scripts, override headers, rewrite URLs, and block crawlers using their own defaults.

The critical insight: a site can score 100% in a clean test environment and drop to 81% in production because of what the infrastructure layer adds after deploy. That gap is entirely invisible if you only audit the code you wrote.

This is the most common source of "inexplicable" score drops. The code didn't change. The environment did.

These are patterns we've documented firsthand on live properties - not hypotheticals:

Cloudflare Bot Management (Browser Integrity Check): When enabled, CF injects a challenge script from cdn-cgi/challenge-platform/ on every page load. This script is render-blocking and uncontrolled - it dropped Best Practices from 100 to 92 and Performance from 100 to 81 on multiple sites. The code was clean. CF was the cause.

Cloudflare Email Obfuscation: If your pages have mailto links or plain email addresses, CF's "Scrape Shield" feature injects email-decode.min.js - a render-blocking script that adds ~150ms to page load on every visit. Enabled by default on most Cloudflare plans.

CF Managed robots.txt: Cloudflare's "Manage your robots.txt" feature injects a Content-Signal header that reduces SEO scores to 92. It operates separately from the "Block AI bots" setting - you can disable one without affecting the other.

Stale CDN cache after deploy: Edge caches don't always clear immediately after a new deploy. Visitors receive old JS and CSS for hours or days after you've pushed a fix. Score improvements that are real in testing don't show up in production until cache purge propagates.

Third-party script updates: Analytics, chat widgets, and review platforms update their embed scripts without notice. A script that passed last month's audit may have added 80KB or a new fingerprinting API this month.

BIC: BP 92 → 81 Email Obfuscation: +150ms LCP robots.txt header: SEO 92 CSP + Turnstile: BP 92

You audit the live environment, not just the code. Every audit we run starts with a curl against the live domain before we look at a single line of source. We're checking what the domain actually delivers - injected scripts, headers, redirects - not what the repo says it should deliver.

A developer who only audits their local build or staging environment will miss every one of these. They're invisible until you curl the production URL and grep for what shouldn't be there.

This is why "my developer just fixed it" sometimes doesn't change the score. If the fix was applied to code but the infrastructure-level issue wasn't addressed, the score won't move.

Part 04

Why you need
ongoing monitoring - at least 30-60 days.

Because the live environment continues to change after launch - without you doing anything. CDN edge caches take time to fully propagate new rules. Cloudflare periodically rolls out new default features (bot protection, image optimization, analytics) that can inject behavior into your pages. Google re-crawls on its own schedule and may start seeing things differently on the second or third visit.

The first 30 days after a new site goes live are when the environment settles. Cache layers stabilize. CF's monitoring tools start accumulating data about your domain and may trigger new rules. Real-device variance - the difference between a Pixel 6 on 4G in Dallas and the PSI test environment - becomes visible in the score data.

Launch day scores are a snapshot of one environment on one day. Monitoring scores over 30 days gives you a baseline of what real users actually experience.

Here's a real sequence we've observed: Site launches at 100%. Two weeks later, Best Practices drops to 92. No code changed. Investigation finds that Cloudflare's Browser Integrity Check was auto-enabled on the account - possibly by a security recommendation notification the owner dismissed without reading. The injected challenge script is now loading on every page.

This is not hypothetical. It's the exact scenario that prompted our three-step CF nuclear fix protocol: disable browser check, set security to essentially off, disable bot management JS via the API. Three commands. Score restored. But only if you're watching closely enough to catch the drop in the first place.

A site with no monitoring has no way of knowing when this happens. The scores quietly drift while the owner assumes everything is fine.

After the environment has settled, the risk profile shifts from infrastructure-layer surprises to application-layer drift. Browser security requirements tighten roughly quarterly - what passes today may flag six months from now. Third-party embeds update their scripts. A new page gets added without following the build standard.

All client builds include an automated weekly audit that runs every Monday and stores results to a dashboard you can check any time. It won't catch everything - some CF-level injections require a live curl check - but it catches score movement before it compounds.

The recommended cadence after initial stabilization: review the weekly audit monthly, run a full manual audit any time you add pages, integrate a third party, or change hosting configuration. That's it.

Weekly automated audit Score stored to dashboard Catches drift before it compounds

Any of these should prompt a full re-audit, not just a score check:

DNS or hosting changes - transferring a domain, switching nameservers, or adding a CDN layer can wipe security headers and redirect configurations you thought were permanent.

Third-party integrations - adding a chat widget, booking system, payment embed, or analytics tool. Each one is a new script loading on your domain with its own headers, CSP conflicts, and performance budget.

New pages or sections - especially if someone other than the original developer added them. New content added outside the build standard will rarely meet it by accident.

A score drop you notice - even one category, even a few points. Score drops are symptoms of a specific cause. Ignoring them means the cause is still running.
